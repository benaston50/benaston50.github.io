COVID-19 and the Cost of Living in England

Aims of my Project

England has, in recent months, been plagued by a Cost of Living crisis. This includes a large level of inflation, major increases in energy prices and wage increases which do not reflect such financial strain. By undertaking this project, I aim to discover if the COVID-19 Pandemic has contributed to England's current Cost of Living crisis, and if so to what extent.

I aim to examine different factors, such as the changes in various aspects of the Cost of Living, how employment and earnings have changed and regional and societal variations in impact.

How hard did COVID-19 hit each region of England?

Before analysing which regions of England are most impacted by the Cost of Living crisis, I decided it would be beneficial to see the impact of COVID-19 on regions, allowing me to draw comparisons later between regions in terms of COVID-19 effect and the cost of living.

As is visible in the chart, the North West and the South East had the highest number of coronavirus cases. This is likely due to the multiple major cities in these areas such as London, Manchester and Liverpool.

However, due to higher populations in these areas this does not necessarily reflect the true impact of COVID-19 on individuals, so I then found the number of people per positive COVID test in each region.

This shows that COVID-19 levels were roughly similar in all regions but the South West and West Midlands had the greatest per-person COVID rates, which could indicate COVID had a higher impact in these areas.

I retrieved this data from the ONS website and used an open source GeoJSON Generator to create the map of England. I could not immediately use the data and had to clean and standardise it first, and struggled initially to match region identities in both the map files and the data file. After analysing both files, I managed to fix all inconsistencies to get all regions functioning.

How does the Cost of Living compare between regions?

By comparing energy costs across regions through COVID, I can see to what extent each region is impacted by COVID.

This graph shows that energy prices have shot up. It also indicates that the impact of the Cost of Living crisis is fairly consistent across regions, showing the regional cases of COVID had little to no impact on energy prices. This could be because energy prices were raised by an outside shock, or because restrictions were consistent across all regions.

For this graph, I attempted to scrape data from a 3rd party site using different methods as seen on my colab, but the site had restrictions that I could not bypass with pandas or BeautifulSoup, so I opted to copy the table myself. As the data was very limited, I opted not to use it and instead use the public time series data from gov.uk.

What are key metrics for the Cost of Living?

I believed it would give me a helpful insight into the Cost of Living crisis by looking into key metrics relating to inflation, both CPI and RPI related. I also wanted to compare this to changes in labour costs to see how worker pay has changed.

Multiple of these graphs, such as the CPI index, CPI energy weighting and RPI leisure goods weighting saw a shock around COVID.

How does Inflation relate to Unemployment?

Next, I wanted to analyse if inflation had a relation to unemployment, as COVID caused businesses to lay off many staff.

I also used Python code to run a regression AI.

This showed the expected relationship. CPI and Unemployment are statistically significant with an R-Squared value of 0.438, meanining lower levels of unemployment leads to higher inflation levels. However, by hovering over the graph you can see many of the high points with low unemployment are recent, showing there is not lasting unemployment due to COVID and is instead a desire for workers from companies. Conclusions

Through the graphs I have analysed in this project, I have come to the conclusion that the disruptions caused through COVID-19 are not significantly at fault for causing the current Cost of Living crisis, although it could have had minor impacts as a supporting factor to create a volatile economy.
